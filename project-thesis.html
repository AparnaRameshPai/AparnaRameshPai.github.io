Overview

What this research is about

An equilibrium model of the Nordic power sector is adapted to model nuclear plants' flexibility as a resource stock to be deployed over the year. The study investigates how treating nuclear fuel cycles as a dispatchable resource — rather than a fixed baseload — changes our understanding of nuclear power's role in a grid with high renewable energy penetration.

The Nordic electricity market is a compelling context for this question: it combines hydro-dominated generation, significant wind expansion, and a mix of nuclear capacities across Sweden and Finland, creating unique dynamics around flexibility and pricing.

Primary research question
"How can modelling of nuclear fuel cycles as a resource stock deployed over the year impact the assessment of nuclear-power-plant flexibility and the overall equilibrium of the Nordic electricity market under high VRE penetration?"

Methodology

Approach & methods

This thesis develops a deterministic partial-equilibrium model of the Swedish power system to investigate how modelling nuclear fuel cycles as a depletable resource stock — analogous to a hydropower reservoir — affects assessments of nuclear flexibility under high variable renewable energy (VRE) penetration.

The model covers Sweden's four bidding zones at hourly resolution across a full year, formulated as a quadratic programme maximising social welfare under perfect competition and implemented in GAMS using the CPLEX solver. Two model variants are compared across 2030, 2040, and 2050 VRE scenarios: a conventional ramp-rate-only representation and a novel fuel-cycle formulation incorporating both a finite fuel-energy stock constraint and a cycling-operations budget.

The shadow prices on these constraints are interpreted as the fuel value and flexibility value respectively — direct analogues to the water value in hydropower scheduling — providing a new lens for quantifying the economic cost of nuclear flexibility in hydro-dominated systems.

Findings

Results & insights

Work in progress — results are preliminary and subject to change

The analysis compares three model calibrations — Model R (ramp-rate only), Model F Old, and Model F New (fuel-cycle constrained) — across nuclear generation, zonal prices, and fuel scarcity premiums. Three early findings stand out from the baseline calibration.

Finding 1 — Nuclear overestimation
Model R over-generates at 68.1 TWh, exceeding the 2018 observed value of 63 TWh. Imposing a fuel budget in Model F New brings output exactly to 63 TWh, suggesting ramp-rate-only models systematically overestimate nuclear generation.
Finding 2 — Fuel scarcity premium
Model F New yields a fuel scarcity value of 1.42 EUR/MWh per TWh constrained — a cleaner estimate of the opportunity cost of burning fuel today versus saving it for higher-price periods, directly analogous to the water value in hydro scheduling.
Finding 3 — Price effects & network impact
The fuel constraint raises average prices by ~20% over Model R (42.90 vs 35.66 EUR/MWh). SE4 prices nearly converge with SE1–SE3 under the fuel-cycle model, hinting that nuclear constraints subtly reduce north–south congestion pressure.
Scenario analysis
Comparative results across 2030, 2040, and 2050 VRE penetration scenarios are ongoing. Full findings will be available upon thesis completion.
