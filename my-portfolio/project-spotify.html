Overview

Can you predict a hit by its sound?

Can you predict how popular a song will be just by analysing its sound? This project explores that question using Spotify's dataset of 114,000 tracks across multiple genres.

Rather than following a predefined direction, this is an open-ended data investigation — exploring patterns, challenging assumptions, and letting the data tell its own story. The analysis combines exploratory data analysis, statistical hypothesis testing, and machine learning to uncover what truly drives a song's popularity.

Research questions

What I set out to find

1
Which audio features correlate most with popularity?
2
Are happy songs more popular than sad ones?
3
What separates a hit from a flop?
4
Can audio features alone predict popularity?

Key findings

What the data showed

1 — Audio features barely predict popularity
No single audio feature correlates above 0.10 with popularity — essentially zero on a scale of -1 to +1. The strongest predictor is instrumentalness (-0.10), suggesting vocal-led songs perform marginally better. Sound alone is almost no indicator of success.
2 — Sad songs outperform happy songs
Contrary to intuition, sad songs (valence ≤ 0.5) average a popularity score of 34.21 vs happy songs at 32.08. A t-test confirms this is statistically significant (p = 0.0000) — however the practical difference is only 2.14 points on a 100-point scale. This finding highlights an important distinction: statistical significance ≠ practical significance.
3 — Hits are louder and more vocal-led
Comparing the top 10% (hits, 63+ popularity) vs bottom 10% (flops, 0 popularity): hit songs are on average 1 dB louder than flops, have significantly lower instrumentalness (0.057 vs 0.103), and are less acoustic (0.255 vs 0.341). The biggest separator is loudness — but even this difference is small.
4 — Even machine learning struggles
ModelR² ScoreRMSE
Linear Regression 0.020 22.05
Random Forest 0.550 14.94

Even a Random Forest combining all audio features explains only 55% of popularity variance — with predictions still off by ~15 points on average. Nearly half of what drives popularity remains unexplained by sound alone.

Central insight

The real conclusion

What makes a song popular cannot be heard — it can only be felt through the cultural moment it lands in.

Artist fame, playlist placement, social media virality, and timing explain far more than danceability, energy, or tempo ever could. You can engineer the perfect sounding song — but you cannot engineer a hit.
